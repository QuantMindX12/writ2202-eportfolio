The Challenge

For our major research project, my team tackled a concern we all shared: the over-reliance on AI-generated code. We designed an experiment to find out how reliably ChatGPT-5 could write Python code for data cleaning and visualization.

We weren't looking for obvious crashes. We searched for "silent logic errors": code that produces plausible results without alerting you that the numbers are wrong. As a future data scientist, I've learned that these are potentially the most dangerous errors.

Part 1: How We Worked Together

In previous group projects, disorganization often led to stress. This time, we started by developing a "Code of Conduct"—a contract outlining how we would respect each other's time.

My role was The Progress Coordinator. My priority was ensuring our work met the rubric and stayed integrated as a whole.

Key Learnings on Collaboration:

  • Boundaries: We set "Quiet Hours" (11:15 PM - 7:15 AM) so everyone could rest.
  • Accountability: A strike system meant we stayed on track without ever needing to use it.
  • Trust: Effective teamwork is about enforced boundaries and mutual trust, not just being friendly.

Part 2: Planning the Experiment

At the outset, our project goals were too broad. I guided my team in using a decision matrix to narrow our experiment to one practical question: Can ChatGPT-5 reliably clean and visualize a CSV file?

1. The Setup

We defined a standardized prompt and prepared a test CSV (`employee_records.csv`) with known "ground truth" values for mean age and bonuses.

2. Data Generation

We submitted the prompt 5 times in new chat sessions to test variability. We treated ChatGPT as a "black box."

3. The Audit

We measured success not just by "did it run?" but "is the math right?" (Calculation Accuracy) and "is the graph readable?" (Visualization Success).

Planning Insight

"We initially considered tools like Grammarly and Quillbot before focusing solely on ChatGPT-5. This experience taught me the value of saying 'no' to some good ideas to achieve real depth on the best one."

Final Reflections

Redefining "Soft Skills"

Before WRIT 2202, I thought soft skills were just a resume item. I now see that clear communication, structured planning, and regular auditing are central to good data science work. Our ground truth checks were crucial; without them, we could have accepted wrong answers from the AI without even realizing it.

The Data Scientist as a "Translator"

This course taught me that a Data Scientist must be a translator. It is not enough to find errors; I must be able to explain their impact to stakeholders who do not write code.

A specific example of this was when our team had to define 'silent logic errors' for our audience. I had to find a way to explain that a bar chart could look completely polished and professional while actually being statistically wrong. Learning to articulate this distinction—between looking right and being right—showed me how to bridge the gap between technical data and non-technical decision-makers.

Looking Forward

Moving forward, I am committed to being the person who challenges assumptions: "How do we know this is right?" I will bring these auditing and teamwork skills to every project, ensuring both technical rigor and an ethical, collaborative team culture.
